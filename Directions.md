## The gap and your contributions

State the gap as the intersection of two omissions, not one. On the options side, the BTC RND/VRP/pricing-kernel work — Almeida, Grith & Miftachov recover Bitcoin risk-neutral densities from Deribit and find a W-shaped pricing kernel that is steep in negative returns, with risk premia varying across two volatility regimes, and Atanasova et al. recover the risk-neutral distribution from a large cross-section of Deribit options and find a positive, highly persistent variance risk premium with economically important higher-order risks — all need a *physical* density, and they get it from backward-looking realized returns, which is the noisy, small-effective-sample, unstable-higher-moment part of the exercise (Atanasova et al. explicitly motivate their design by the instability of estimating the physical distribution directly). On the prediction-market side, the calibration literature studies binary categorical contracts; even the most recent large-scale work (a 2026 study of 292M trades across Kalshi and Polymarket showing calibration is a structured, multidimensional phenomenon) does not reconstruct a continuous asset-price density.

So frame four contributions:

1. **Methodological.** A direct, assumption-light reconstruction of a *continuous* BTC density from exhaustive range markets. Polymarket range buckets are (discretized) Arrow–Debreu / state-contingent claims: a bucket [K₁,K₂] paying $1 if S_T lands inside is exactly a digital range, so the *price is the bucket's probability mass*. You read the density off directly, whereas from Deribit you must apply Breeden–Litzenberger (1978) and difference an interpolated call surface twice — the numerically delicate step the whole RND literature struggles with. Your density needs no strike interpolation, no smoothing kernel, no SVI fit.

2. **Forecasting.** The first systematic calibration/sharpness/proper-scoring evaluation of a continuous asset-price density forecast extracted from a prediction market — extending the categorical-event calibration literature to asset prices.

3. **Asset pricing (the headline).** Polymarket gives you a *forward-looking, market-based proxy for the physical measure P*, which is precisely the weak link in the BTC pricing-kernel/VRP literature. That lets you estimate the variance risk premium and the empirical pricing kernel with a forward-looking P instead of realized returns, and ask whether the W-shaped-kernel result survives.

4. **Microstructure.** Cross-venue integration and price discovery between a DeFi prediction market and a centralized options exchange.

## The conceptual core: which measure does Polymarket give you?

This is the spine of the paper and you should resolve it explicitly, because it's where reviewers will push. Deribit options are spanned by a tradeable underlying (perp/spot), so no-arbitrage pins them to the risk-neutral measure **Q** (approximately, given crypto frictions). Polymarket contracts settle in USDC with no built-in replication against BTC, so they do *not* inherit Q the same way. But they don't give clean **P** either: Manski (2006) showed that with risk-neutral, budget-constrained, heterogeneous-belief traders, the contract price is a particular quantile of the budget-weighted belief distribution — it does not reveal the mean belief, only a bound on it, and Wolfers & Zitzewitz show that with risk aversion and wealth constraints prices are biased toward ½, producing a favorite–longshot bias.

So Polymarket gives a third object — call it a budget/belief-weighted subjective measure **M** — that is plausibly *closer to P than Q is* (no systematic insurance/hedging demand inflating tails) but is itself distorted (favorite-longshot, funding costs). The clean payoff: **Part 1 is what licenses Part 2.** If your reconstructed M is well-calibrated to realized BTC outcomes (good PIT, low CRPS), that is empirical evidence M ≈ P, which then justifies treating Q/M as an empirical pricing kernel in Part 3. Make that logical dependency the organizing argument of the paper. The Q-vs-M wedge you measure is not a pure SDF; it bundles risk premia, M's belief distortions, and cross-venue frictions, and disentangling those is the work.

## Phased plan

**Phase 0 — Data and alignment.** Note that Polymarket runs two relevant formats: exhaustive range markets ("What price will Bitcoin hit in June?") that give you a PMF directly, and threshold markets ("Bitcoin above ___ on June 4?") that give you the CDF/survival function — both reconstructable, the range format being cleaner. Pull order-book snapshots (mid, last, VWAP, depth) at fixed times-to-resolution. From Deribit pull the option chain plus the Deribit index and settlement convention. Critically align resolution timing and reference index: Deribit settles on its own index at a fixed UTC time; Polymarket has its own resolution source and fixing — any mismatch is a basis you must control for, not ignore.

**Phase 1 — Reconstruct and characterize the distribution.** Normalize bucket prices (they won't sum to one; the deviation is an *overround* analogous to a bookmaker's vig and is itself informative — report it as a liquidity/integration cost). Build the CDF, fit a parametric tail (generalized Pareto or lognormal) to the open-ended end buckets so you can compute moments. Run internal no-arbitrage checks: non-negative implied density, monotone CDF, Σπ ≤ 1 + costs; the violation frequency is a clean market-quality metric.

**Phase 2 — Calibration as a probabilistic forecast.** Use the Diebold–Gunther–Tay PIT framework: compute F(realized S_T) per market; under correct calibration the PITs are Uniform(0,1). The PIT-histogram shape diagnoses the failure mode — U-shaped means the density is too narrow (overconfident tails too thin), ∩-shaped means too wide. Add proper scoring rules (CRPS for the full density, log score, Brier for buckets) and a reliability diagram on the bucket contracts to test favorite-longshot directly. **Crucial econometric caveat:** daily markets have overlapping horizons and a path-dependent underlying, so PITs/scores are serially dependent — use block bootstrap or HAC inference, or restrict to non-overlapping markets. Condition on volatility regime.

**Phase 3 — Implied volatility and the Deribit comparison.** For an apples-to-apples comparison, don't just report a standard deviation. Reconstruct synthetic call prices C(K) = DF·E_M[(S_T−K)⁺] from your density and invert Black–Scholes strike-by-strike to get a *full Polymarket-implied smile*, then overlay it on Deribit's. Separately, compute a "Polymarket BVIX" using the same model-free variance-swap formula the literature applies to Deribit (Alexander & Imeraj's geometric-variance-swap construction, the CBOE VIX methodology applied to Bitcoin options) so the two volatility indices are constructed identically. Then study the wedge as a function of time-to-resolution. Here you have a sharp pre-registered hypothesis from the prediction-market side: Page & Clemen find that prediction-market miscalibration toward favorite-longshot bias grows with time to expiration, with prices reasonably calibrated only at short horizons — so you should predict that the Polymarket-vs-Deribit wedge *shrinks as time-to-resolution → 0*, and that any excess smile convexity (fat-tail mispricing) is strongest far from resolution.

## Going deeper: theoretical observations and extensions on the options comparison

These are framed as testable propositions, each with the fact about Polymarket it would reveal.

1. **Empirical pricing kernel with a forward-looking P (your strongest extension).** Form Q/M projected onto returns and compare its shape to the W-shaped pricing kernel that the existing literature obtains using a realized-return physical density. The question that makes this publishable: is the pricing-kernel puzzle (non-monotonicity) robust to a forward-looking P, or is part of it an artifact of the noisy backward-looking P estimate? The existing BTC pricing-kernel work derives the kernel as the ratio of risk-neutral to physical density and treats it as revealing time-varying investor preferences — you're proposing a better-measured denominator.

2. **A model-light variance risk premium.** BVIX²_Deribit − Var_M(returns)/T is a VRP estimate that needs no model of P. Compare its magnitude and persistence to the large positive BTC variance risk premium (reported around 0.14 annualized, far above the S&P 500's ~2%) that the literature obtains using realized variance. How much survives when P is forward-looking and forward-looking?

3. **Cross-venue static arbitrage and the law of one price.** A Polymarket bucket equals a Deribit butterfly/call-spread. Net of all costs (Deribit fees, Polymarket fees, USDC funding, gas), measure deviation frequency, size, and persistence. This is your market-integration test, and the persistence speaks to limits-to-arbitrage. Note this is currently done only crudely — a hobbyist write-up frames it as exporting Deribit's volatility-model "knowledge" into the inefficient Polymarket order book, with no rigorous treatment of costs, measure, or persistence.

4. **Price discovery / lead-lag.** Hasbrouck information shares or Gonzalo–Granger decomposition between Polymarket density moments, Deribit implied moments, and spot. Does retail-sentiment-driven Polymarket lead or lag professional options flow?

5. **Favorite-longshot bias on a price axis.** Almost all FLB evidence is for categorical contracts. Testing whether *extreme price buckets* are systematically overpriced relative to both realized frequency and Q — and whether this is horizon-dependent — would document FLB in a continuous asset-price market, which is novel. It maps directly to excess smile convexity (implied excess kurtosis) in M relative to Q.

6. **Embedded discount/funding rate.** Near-certain buckets priced below $1 reveal an implied discount factor over the horizon; back it out and compare to USDC lending / crypto funding rates. This isolates a cost-of-capital wedge specific to DeFi prediction markets and is a clean, citable side result.

7. **Directional/skew sentiment.** Compare M-implied skew to Deribit skew. Crypto options sometimes show upside (call) skew unlike equities; if Polymarket retail show even more right-tail mass, that's evidence of upside-chasing / lottery preference among prediction-market participants.

8. **Higher-moment term structure.** Track how implied skew and kurtosis of M versus Q evolve as time-to-resolution shrinks, complementing the volatility term-structure comparison.

# More on the pricing kernel 


**What a pricing kernel is, plainly.** There are two different probabilities for any future event, and they usually disagree. There's the *real-world* probability — how often something actually happens (call it P). And there's the *market-priced* probability baked into option prices (call it Q). These differ because people pay extra to be protected against bad outcomes. The pricing kernel is just the ratio of the two, state by state: how much more (or less) the market "charges" for a dollar delivered in each possible future state than that state's real-world likelihood would justify. It's the fingerprint of investors' risk attitudes.

An analogy: fire insurance. Maybe your house has a 0.5% real chance of burning down this year, but the insurance is priced as if it were 1%, because people will pay a premium to be covered in that bad state. The ratio — priced 1% versus true 0.5% — is the kernel for the "fire" state: it says people value a dollar in the fire state about twice as much as its raw odds warrant. For Bitcoin it's the same logic: option prices overweight crash states relative to how often crashes truly happen, because traders pay up for crash protection. The kernel maps out that overweighting across every possible future BTC price.

**Why estimating it is hard, and where the bottleneck is.** To compute the kernel you need *both* densities at the same future date. Q is the easy half — you read it straight off today's option prices; it's forward-looking and market-based. The hard half is P, the real-world distribution. The standard trick is to estimate P from *history*: look at how BTC has actually moved over many past windows and fit a distribution. That P has four problems:

- It's backward-looking — it assumes the future resembles the past, which is rough for crypto where regimes flip fast.
- It's measured under different information than Q. Q reflects what the market expects *right now*; historical P is an average over past conditions. You're dividing two numbers that were never measured at the same moment.
- The sample is thin. To get a clean 30-day-ahead distribution you need many *non-overlapping* 30-day windows, i.e. years of data — over which the world changed. The tails and skewness are especially noisy.
- Because the kernel is a *ratio*, any noise in that shaky denominator gets amplified.

This matters because the famous result in the BTC literature is that the kernel comes out W-shaped — non-monotonic — instead of the smooth downward slope basic theory predicts. A real worry is that some of that weird shape is an *artifact of the bad historical P estimate*, not a true feature of investor preferences.

**What Polymarket changes.** A Polymarket BTC range market is a crowd of traders, today, with today's information, putting real money on where BTC will sit on a specific future date — and you can make that date the *same* date the options expire. So it gives you a forward-looking, market-based estimate of P that is matched to Q in every way the historical method fails to match: same moment, same information set, same horizon, same skin-in-the-game character. Now the ratio Q/P is a genuine apples-to-apples comparison at a point in time, rather than something stitched together from years of stale history.

A concrete version: suppose today Deribit options imply a 12% chance BTC falls below $80k in 30 days (that's Q). Historical returns might say 8%, but that's averaged over old regimes. Meanwhile the Polymarket "below $80k" bucket trades at 9¢, so today's real-money crowd says ≈9% (your P proxy). The kernel in that crash state is then about 12/9 ≈ 1.33 — investors are valuing a dollar in that crash state about a third more than its actuarial worth, and you've measured that *cleanly and contemporaneously* instead of inferring it from a noisy historical fit. Do this across all buckets and you trace out the whole kernel, then ask: does the W-shape survive a better P? That question — is the puzzle real or a measurement artifact — is the contribution.

**The honest caveat.** Polymarket doesn't hand you pure P. Its prices carry their own distortions — favorite-longshot bias, the cost of locking up collateral, retail quirks — so it's a *proxy* for P, not P itself. That's exactly why the calibration work in Part 1 isn't a side dish: if you show the Polymarket density is well-calibrated to what BTC actually does (good PIT, low CRPS), you've earned the right to treat it as a credible P, and the kernel estimate stands on that. If it's poorly calibrated, the kernel you get is telling you about Polymarket's biases rather than about investor preferences — which is itself a finding, just a different one. Either way, swapping a forward-looking market proxy in for the historical P is the move that makes the kernel estimate better identified than what's currently in the literature.
